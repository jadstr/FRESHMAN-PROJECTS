Frequently Asked Questions:

Generative AI (GenAI) refers to machine-learning models that can produce new content such as text, images, audio, code, video and much more. After learning patterns from large datasets. Examples include large language models (LLMs), image generators and music synthesis systems.
Most GenAI models are trained on huge datasets using neural networks. During training they learn statistical relationships in data. At inference time they generate content by sampling from the learned distribution, guided by prompts or conditioning inputs.
Text: ChatGPT, Claude, Bard. Images: DALL·E, Midjourney, Stable Diffusion. Code assistants: GitHub Copilot. Audio/video: MusicLM, Sora and research prototypes. Many tools combine multiple modalities.
GenAI can accelerate creative tasks, draft and refine text, generate design ideas, synthesize images and audio, assist coding, summarize documents, and automate repetitive content workflows — often producing results faster than manual work.
Limitations include hallucinations (plausible-sounding but incorrect outputs), sensitivity to prompt wording, bias in training data, copyright and attribution concerns, privacy risks, and misuse (disinformation, deepfakes). Models also require substantial compute and careful evaluation.
Artificial Intelligence can become dangerous in several ways. If AI systems are designed or used irresponsibly, they can lead to unintended consequences such as biased decision-making, privacy violations, and job displacement. Additionally, advanced AI systems could be exploited for malicious purposes, such as creating deepfakes or automating cyberattacks. There is also the risk of losing control over highly autonomous AI systems, which could act in ways that are misaligned with human values and interests. Ensuring robust safety measures, ethical guidelines, and regulatory oversight is crucial to mitigate these risks.
